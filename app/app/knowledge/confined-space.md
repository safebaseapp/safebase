# Confined Space Entry

## Overview

A confined space is a space large enough for a worker to enter, has limited or restricted means of entry or exit, and is not designed for continuous occupancy.

A permit-required confined space may contain hazardous atmospheres, engulfment hazards, internal configurations that can trap or asphyxiate workers, or other serious safety hazards.

## Main Hazards

- Oxygen deficiency or enrichment
- Toxic gases or vapors
- Flammable or explosive atmospheres
- Engulfment
- Restricted entry and exit
- Mechanical or electrical energy
- Heat stress
- Falling objects
- Poor visibility
- Difficult emergency rescue

## Main Recommendations

- Identify and evaluate the confined space before entry.
- Determine whether the space is permit-required.
- Prevent unauthorized entry.
- Complete an approved confined space entry permit.
- Isolate the space from hazardous energy and process materials.
- Apply lockout and tagout where required.
- Blank, blind, disconnect, or isolate connected lines where necessary.
- Test the atmosphere before entry.
- Monitor atmospheric conditions during entry when required.
- Ventilate the space adequately.
- Establish acceptable entry conditions.
- Assign a trained attendant outside the space.
- Maintain communication with entrants.
- Control access to the entry point.
- Provide suitable lighting and safe access.
- Prepare rescue arrangements before entry begins.
- Stop entry immediately if unsafe conditions develop.

## Atmospheric Testing

- Test oxygen concentration.
- Test for flammable gases and vapors.
- Test for toxic gases and contaminants.
- Test the atmosphere in the correct sequence.
- Use calibrated and suitable gas detection equipment.
- Record test results on the entry permit.
- Continue monitoring where conditions may change.
- Do not rely on smell to determine atmospheric safety.

## Entry Team Responsibilities

### Authorized Entrant

- Understand the hazards of the space.
- Use required PPE and entry equipment.
- Maintain communication with the attendant.
- Leave the space immediately when ordered or when unsafe conditions occur.

### Attendant

- Remain outside the confined space.
- Track authorized entrants.
- Monitor conditions inside and outside the space.
- Prevent unauthorized entry.
- Order evacuation when required.
- Summon rescue services without entering unless specifically trained and authorized.

### Entry Supervisor

- Confirm that entry conditions are acceptable.
- Verify that permits, tests, equipment, and rescue arrangements are complete.
- Authorize entry.
- Cancel the permit when work is completed or conditions become unsafe.

## Rescue and Emergency Arrangements

- Establish rescue arrangements before entry.
- Confirm that rescue personnel can respond in time.
- Provide suitable retrieval equipment where appropriate.
- Ensure rescue personnel are trained and equipped.
- Do not allow unplanned or impulsive rescue entry.
- Use non-entry rescue methods where practical.

## Required PPE and Equipment

- Safety helmet
- Safety footwear
- Suitable gloves
- Eye and face protection
- Protective clothing
- Respiratory protection where required
- Full-body harness where retrieval is necessary
- Tripod or suitable retrieval system where applicable
- Calibrated gas detector
- Communication equipment
- Suitable lighting
- Ventilation equipment

## Applicable Standards

- OSHA 29 CFR 1910.146 – Permit-Required Confined Spaces
- OSHA 29 CFR 1926 Subpart AA – Confined Spaces in Construction
- OSHA 29 CFR 1926.1203 – General Requirements
- OSHA 29 CFR 1926.1204 – Permit-Required Confined Space Program
- OSHA 29 CFR 1926.1206 – Entry Permit
- OSHA 29 CFR 1926.1211 – Rescue and Emergency Services

## Best Practices

- Use a confined space entry checklist.
- Verify isolation before issuing the permit.
- Conduct a pre-entry briefing.
- Test gas conditions at different levels within the space.
- Keep the entry point clear.
- Review simultaneous operations near the space.
- Revalidate conditions after breaks or work interruptions.
- Cancel the permit when conditions change.

## Important Notes

- A permit alone does not make entry safe.
- Atmospheric testing does not replace isolation.
- The attendant must not perform duties that interfere with monitoring entrants.
- Rescue arrangements must be realistic and available before entry.
- Hot work inside a confined space requires additional controls.
